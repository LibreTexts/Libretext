The groups \({\mathbb Z}_8\) and \({\mathbb Z}_{12}\) cannot be isomorphic since they have different orders; however, it is true that \(U(8) \cong U(12)\text{.}\) We know that

\begin{align*} U(8) & = \{1, 3, 5, 7 \}\\ U(12) & = \{1, 5, 7, 11 \}\text{.} \end{align*}

An isomorphism \(\phi : U(8) \rightarrow U(12)\) is then given by

\begin{align*} 1 & \mapsto 1\\ 3 & \mapsto 5\\ 5 & \mapsto 7\\ 7 & \mapsto 11\text{.} \end{align*}

The map \(\phi\) is not the only possible isomorphism between these two groups. We could define another isomorphism \(\psi\) by \(\psi(1) = 1\text{,}\) \(\psi(3) = 11\text{,}\) \(\psi(5) = 5\text{,}\) \(\psi(7) = 7\text{.}\) In fact, both of these groups are isomorphic to \({\mathbb Z}_2 \times {\mathbb Z}_2\) (see Example 3.3.5 in Chapter 3).
