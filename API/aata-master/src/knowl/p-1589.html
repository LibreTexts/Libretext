We now condense all this information into the statement that “Uis the internal direct product of Aand B.” By Theorem 9.2.15, we see that Uis isomorphic to a product of a cyclic group of order \(6\) and a cyclic group of order \(2\text{.}\) So in a very real sense, Uis no more or less complicated than \({\mathbb Z}_6\times{\mathbb Z}_2\text{,}\) which is in turn isomorphic to \({\mathbb Z}_3\times{\mathbb Z}_2\times{\mathbb Z}_2\text{.}\) So we totally understand the “structure” of U. For example, we can see that Uis not cyclic, since when written as a product of cyclic groups, the two orders are not relatively prime.
